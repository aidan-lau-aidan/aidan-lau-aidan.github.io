Stochastic Homogenization

This page is a very short overview of the classical ideas of stochastic homogenization. The goal is to provide some intuition, a visual, and some links to much better explanations.

The basic intuition in stochastic homogenization is that of an inhomogeneous material. We imagine that there is a background which conducts heat unevenly in different places, and model this environment by a stationary, matrix-valued, random coefficient field \(\a(\cdot)\). Stationarity means that for every shift \(y\), the random fields \(\mathbf a(\cdot)\) and \(\mathbf a(\cdot+y)\) have the same law. In other words, the field is statistically homogeneous: it looks the same everywhere in space. In order to see deterministic large-scale behaviour, we assume in addition that the law of the coefficient field is ergodic (averaging occurs, via the ergodic theorem). However, conductivity itself is not a directly observable quantity. Instead we track the position of a diffusing particle or the flow of heat in the material, which leads us to consider the homogenization of a specific PDE.

The basic mathematical setup is the following. Suppose that \(\a(\cdot)\) is an \(\R^d\)-stationary, ergodic, uniformly elliptic, matrix-valued random field. We want to find a constant, deterministic, effective matrix \(\bar{\a}\) such that if for every \(\epsilon\in (0,1) \) we let \(u^\epsilon\) (the heterogeneous solution) and let \(\bar{u}\) (the homogenized solution) be the unique solutions (for nice enough \(U,f,g\)) to \[\left\{\begin{aligned} - \nabla \cdot \a(x/\epsilon) \nabla u^\epsilon & = f & \mbox{ in } U\\ u^\epsilon & = g & \mbox{ on } \partial U \end{aligned} \right. \quad \quad \left\{\begin{aligned} - \nabla \cdot \bar{\a} \nabla \bar{u} & = f & \mbox{ in } U\\ \bar{u} & = g & \mbox{ on } \partial U \end{aligned} \right. \] then almost surely with respect to the randomness of the coefficient field \[ \lim_{\epsilon \to 0}\|u^\epsilon - \bar{u} \|_{L^2(U)} = 0\,. \] Motivated by the "two-scale expansion", the classical homogenization theory states that the homogenized matrix \(\bar{\a}\) is constructed as a corrected ergodic averaging of the coefficient field. That is, for every \(e\in \R^d\) there exists a unique stationary gradient field \(\nabla \phi_e\) solving \[ \nabla \cdot \a (e + \nabla \phi_e) = 0\,, \] and the homogenized matrix is then defined by \[\bar{\a}e = \mathbb{E}[\a (e + \nabla \phi_e)]\,. \]

Since elliptic operators are the generators of diffusion processes, we can also consider convergence of the diffusion processes. If \(\a= \frac{1}{2}\sigma\sigma^t\) then \(\nabla \cdot \a \nabla\) is the generator corresponding to the SDE \[dX_t = \frac{1}{2}\nabla \cdot (\sigma\sigma^t)(X_t) dt + \sigma(X_t)dB_t \] and \(\nabla \cdot \a(\cdot/ \epsilon) \nabla\) is the generator of the rescaled process \( Y^\epsilon_t = \epsilon X_{t/\epsilon^2}\). Classical homogenization theory then states that there exists a deterministic matrix \(\bar{\a}\) such that if \(\bar{B}_t\) is the Brownian motion with covariance matrix \(2\bar{\a}\) then we have convergence in law of the family of rescaled processes: \[Y^\epsilon_t \overset{\epsilon \to 0}{\Rightarrow} \bar{B}_t\,. \]

It is worth mentioning the two classical cases in which the effective matrix \(\bar{\a}\) can be directly computed:

  • In the one dimensional case the effective matrix is the harmonic mean \(\bar{\a} =\mathbb{E}[\a^{-1}]^{-1}\).
  • In the \(2d\) random checkerboard example (which is \(\Z^2\) stationary), if each square is coloured independently with probability \(1/2\) either black or white and \[\a(x) = \begin{cases} \lambda \mathrm{Id} & \mbox{white squares} \\ \Lambda \mathrm{Id} & \mbox{black squares}\end{cases}\] then the homogenized matrix is the geometric mean \(\bar{\a} = \sqrt{\lambda\Lambda}\mathrm{Id}\).
